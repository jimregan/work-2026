<p style="text-align:justify;margin:0">Archives of speech are widespread, but research in the humanities into their contents is often hampered by the lack of search facilities. Despite advances in automatic speech recognition (ASR) in recent years, it is often still desirable to employ custom ASR systems, specifically adapted to the acoustic and linguistic conditions within the archives, rather than something more general purpose.
</p>

Parliamentary recordings have a long history of use as a source for training data for ASR, but parliamentary recordings are interesting in their own right, for several reasons: they typically cover a large time span, making them good sources for investigation into diachronic changes in the language; they can often bridge the gap between read speech and spontaneous speech: although the speaker typically has a prepared script, they may diverge from it in reaction to another speech or other events in the same session; parliamentary representatives are typically drawn from all regions of a country, yielding dialectal diversity; and the variety of topics covered means that in addition to relatively unemotional speech, there can be speeches full of emotion, or affectations of emotion for rhetorical purposes.

Drawing upon work intended to create custom ASR systems for the purpose of making the Swedish Riksdag archives more easily searchable, we are making efforts to extend the usefulness of the data into other areas—primarily, we are using an additional phoneme-based recognition system to create an acoustically validated lexicon of Swedish pronunciation. It is our aim to extend this to the semi-automated creation of a database for the purpose of acoustic-based phonetic and phonology research, with a particular interest in language change in the 70+ years for which there are recordings of speeches.
