# Comparative Evaluation of Acoustic Feature Extraction Tools for Clinical Speech Analysis

## Citation

Choi, Anna Seo Gyeong, Alexander Richardson, Ryan Partlan, Sunny X. Tang, and Sunghye Cho. "Comparative Evaluation of Acoustic Feature Extraction Tools for Clinical Speech Analysis." arXiv:2506.01129v2, 2025.

## Overview

This paper compares three commonly used acoustic feature extraction toolkits, OpenSMILE, Praat, and Librosa, in the context of clinical speech analysis. The study focuses on speech from people with schizophrenia spectrum disorders and healthy controls, asking whether different toolkits produce consistent acoustic measurements when configured as similarly as possible.

The main conclusion is that some features are highly consistent across tools, but many are not. In particular, the paper shows that similarly named acoustic features can differ substantially depending on the extraction toolkit, even when researchers try to standardize preprocessing and parameter settings. The authors argue that this creates a serious reproducibility issue for clinical speech research.

## Goals of the Study

The paper addresses three main questions:

1. Whether different acoustic toolkits yield consistent feature values.
2. Which features are relatively robust across tools and participant groups.
3. Which toolkits appear reliable enough for use in clinical speech studies.

## Data

The dataset consists of recorded speech from:

- 33 participants with schizophrenia spectrum disorders
- 38 healthy controls

Participants completed several speech-based tasks, including picture description tasks. Recordings were manually time-stamped and annotated.

## Toolkits Compared

The study evaluates:

- OpenSMILE
- Praat, accessed through Parselmouth
- Librosa

The paper emphasizes that these tools were built for somewhat different purposes:

- Praat for detailed phonetic analysis
- OpenSMILE for batch feature extraction in machine learning settings
- Librosa originally for music information retrieval, though now widely used in speech work

## Feature Extraction Setup

To make the comparison as fair as possible, the authors aligned extraction settings across toolkits:

- 16 kHz sampling rate
- 60 ms frame size
- 10 ms hop size
- Hamming window
- no pre-emphasis
- frequency range 0 to 8000 Hz
- F0 search range 55 to 1000 Hz
- silence threshold of -60 dB

Even with this effort, the authors note that the underlying algorithms still differ across tools, especially for pitch tracking and other feature-specific computations.

## Acoustic Features Examined

The study compares several feature families:

- fundamental frequency (F0)
- formants (F1, F2, F3)
- harmonics-to-noise ratio (HNR)
- jitter
- shimmer
- amplitude measures
- MFCCs

The paper explains the clinical relevance of these features, especially for capturing prosody, articulation, voice quality, and spectral characteristics that may differ in schizophrenia.

## Methods

The authors use correlation analysis to compare toolkit outputs feature by feature. They compute Pearson correlations between toolkit pairs and assess statistical significance. They also compare correlations across the schizophrenia and healthy-control groups. In addition, they evaluate how useful certain features are for distinguishing the two groups using ROC analysis and AUC scores.

## Main Findings

### Features with strong agreement

F0 percentile measures were highly consistent across toolkits. Reported correlations were often extremely high, in many cases above 0.96 and sometimes close to 1.0.

This suggests that some summary pitch measures are comparatively robust across extraction frameworks.

### Features with weak or unstable agreement

Several other features showed poor agreement across tools:

- F0 standard deviation often had weak or even negative correlations
- formant values were inconsistent across toolkits
- HNR standard deviation showed poor agreement
- amplitude mean and amplitude variability depended strongly on toolkit
- MFCC agreement varied by coefficient and toolkit pair

These findings indicate that many features that are often treated as straightforward may in fact be highly implementation-dependent.

### Differences across participant groups

The paper also reports that cross-toolkit agreement was not identical in the two participant groups. Some correlation patterns differed significantly between the schizophrenia and healthy-control groups. This means toolkit choice can affect not only raw feature values but also observed group differences.

### Classification results

Among the extracted features, F0 mean, HNR measures, and MFCC1 showed the strongest ability to distinguish schizophrenia spectrum disorder participants from healthy controls, with AUC values above 0.70 and in some cases above 0.75. The paper notes that OpenSMILE-derived features often performed especially well in this regard.

## Interpretation

The paper’s core message is that acoustic feature extraction is not a neutral preprocessing step. Different tools can encode different assumptions, algorithms, and defaults, and these differences can materially change scientific conclusions.

This matters especially in clinical research, where acoustic features are often used because they are interpretable and theoretically grounded. If the extracted values themselves are unstable across tools, then the resulting interpretations become less reliable.

The paper is therefore both a comparative study and a methodological warning. It argues that researchers should not assume that a feature with the same name means the same thing across OpenSMILE, Praat, and Librosa.

## Recommendations from the Paper

The authors recommend:

1. Developing standardized extraction protocols for clinical speech analysis.
2. Reporting toolkit versions, settings, and extraction methods in detail.
3. Cross-validating acoustic findings across tools and datasets before drawing strong conclusions.
4. Encouraging closer collaboration between speech technology researchers, linguists, and clinicians.
5. Being cautious about black-box or poorly interpreted representations in clinical applications.

## Main Takeaway

The study shows that acoustic feature extraction tools can agree very strongly on some measures and disagree sharply on others, even under carefully aligned settings. For clinical speech analysis, this means reproducibility, transparency, and feature validation are essential. Researchers should treat toolkit choice as a substantive methodological decision rather than an interchangeable implementation detail.
