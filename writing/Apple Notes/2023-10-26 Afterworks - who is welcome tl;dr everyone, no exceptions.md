Afterworks - who is welcome? tl;dr: everyone, no exceptions

There have been some murmurs from some members of faculty to the effect that certain people have felt excluded from afterworks. Those of us who regularly participate would typically like to think that we make every effort to not exclude anyone, but, things happen, situations are misread, etc.
If, even in the totally hypothetical and not real, this-sure-as-hell-didn’t-happen situation that one person were to express certain views of a particular kind of “-ism”, to the degree that two other people were left in tears after hearing such views, those two would not then exclude that person. In this totally hypothetical, made up situation. Or pair of, that is.
If that person is welcome, then *you are too*, for *all* values of “you”.

That said, if you absolutely cannot stand to be in the company of someone that you expect to be present, then, of course, it is your absolute right to not put yourself where you do not feel comfortable. But, be aware, in this case the person that’s excluding you is *you*, and if you have a problem with that, then you get to take it up with… *you*. (But maybe consider discussing the matter with that person).

What might be unclear, particularly for people who (perhaps as the result of some sort of disorder, for example) find it difficult to navigate certain types of human interactions, is that there is a somewhat unclear division between:
1. being welcome to join
2. being personally invited, beyond a general group invitation
3. receiving an offer to have drinks purchased for you

In the case of 1, as stated above, everyone is welcome, no exceptions.

In the case of 2, a whole bunch of things can happen that aren’t intentional. For example, if you were seen to leave with a backpack on at a time when people usually go home, most people will typically interpret that as meaning that you’re not going to be around. It has happened that this turned out not to be the case, but the person affected at least understood that a simple misunderstanding had taken place.
It is also the case that it is quite draining to try to make sure an invitation goes out as broadly as possible, and sometimes we don’t always have the energy to do so. It seems like there is a general awareness of where the usual suspects congregate, and it’s a relatively simple matter to just ask “hey, is anyone doing afterworks?”—it has never, not once been the case that plans were suddenly changed because one person showed an interest. If you find that difficult to trust, trust at least that we’re too damned lazy to change plans at the last minute like that.

In the case of 3, this is really more of an individual matter, and there is an element of an unspoken social contract here, too, that maybe is not quite as obvious to some people as it is to most.
If someone picks up a drink for you, you are expected to pay them back (or, at the very least, to *offer to*). A thank you does not go amiss, either. Without these little social niceties, you tend to find that some forms of direct speech will be interpreted as outright rudeness: for example: “just put it in the fridge” (pro tip: you can easily make such statements more polite by rephrasing in the form of a “would you mind…” question).
Where this can become potentially confusing is where the people going out to pick up drinks have other, longstanding, reciprocal arrangements with other people that they have offered to pick up drinks for: “don’t worry about it”, “get me back next time”, etc. are typical phrases that will be heard in such situations. If you don’t have such a reciprocal arrangement with somebody, and if they haven’t said such a thing *to you directly*, then it does not apply. You are expected to pay for the thing they bought for you.
And if you don’t, sooner or later, they will stop asking.
(Similarly, unless you’re particularly good friends with someone, you don’t get to ask them to pick up more than two items: bear in mind that carrying a bunch of glass bottles quickly becomes heavy, potentially unsafe, and they’re probably carrying for more than just themselves and you.)

Note, here, that this does not mean you are not welcome to join, only that you are expected to buy your own drinks. You’re welcome to join the trip to buy them, in fact. Just not welcome to have your drinks paid for. You get paid the same amount we do, you know we can’t afford that.

A simple remedy here is to simply ask “how do I swish you” or, if you genuinely believe that it’s in repayment for something (that the other person may or may not have forgotten about), it’s totally fine to say something like “is this in return for \[X\], or do I swish you?”

Now, none of this is intended to single anybody out, but if you happen to see yourself in this, you may *feel* as if it is. That is not the intention, feel free to discuss it in private if you think otherwise.
It might also seem as though this is something that is generally known; it is not, though there is a regular, small group of people who usually do the systembolaget run, and they do swap notes: “hey, has this person ever paid you back for beer?”, etc.
(This, in turn, can lead to false memories: maybe you did, that one time, repay (or offer to), but the general impression is not in your favour, and quibbling over the difference won’t go in your favour.)
