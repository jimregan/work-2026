
The difference between a phoneme and a phone is primarily the difference between the abstract and the concrete. As a comparison in visual terms: we can think in terms of the colour "green", but if we look at the colour picker in a drawing program [figure] we see millions of possible concrete values, and while we have shared ideas about what "green" is--the colour of grass, perhaps--if two people were given the task of drawing boundaries on the colour picker to demonstrate separate green from everything else, the boundaries will inevitably vary from person to person, and quite likely if the same person made multiple attempts.

A somewhat related idea is the lie-to-children, the sort of gross over-simplification or pure fiction that's used to provide a narrative structure to aid memory. We all know the story about the apple falling on Newton's head, and remember the connection between Newton and gravity because of it. There was no such apple, but the framing is visually evocative and visceral, in addition to being easy to understand, which makes this example stand out most.

So what is a phoneme? 

> A phoneme is what you think you said (or heard), a phone is what you actually said (or heard).

This, of course, is a lie-to-children, but to think of phonemes as the spoken colours of a language isn't too far off. They are categorical, representing the vast varieties of actual sounds, phones, that the speakers of a language consider to be the same, but with the important proviso that these categories must help to distinguish one word from another: "rice" and "rise" are what's called a minimal pair in English -- a pair of words that differ only in one phoneme, but that are audibly distinct. Swedish does not have this phonemic distinction, so these words are naturally confusable for speakers of Swedish.

dog, dock, dug, and duck are all phonemically distinct from each other--for a speaker of English. A speaker of Polish will confuse them, because Polish does not have a distinction between the vowels, and it additionally has final consonant devoicing: speakers of Polish (and German, Russian, and many other languages) tend to change the sounds of consonants 

Much like the boundaries of green in the colour picker: even among 
phonologists, no two people ever seem to agree what the phonemes of a particular language are.
