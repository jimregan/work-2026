# Digital humanities

Conference this spring: the term “digital humanities” is not in favour.
No replacement term decided, however.
(“Digital” is now default, so it’s no longer needed)

Humanist researchers could benefit from language tools, but more outreach/training is required.

Tools are accessible without meetings/planning etc. — users can upload their own data and have them processed.

New tools can be added to the pipeline (plugins?)

It can be hard to balance between shorter term requirements of individual research projects and the longer term requirements of the research infrastructure.
Another difficulty is when the researcher does not understand the details: what they want may not be possible, or not possible within their timeframe; conversely, they may refrain from asking because they think something is difficult that is not.

Semantic searches from a seed wordlist used for tracking the rise of consumerism, to identify when people started to buy items rather than making them themselves.

A lot of interest in diachronic data, tracking change over time.

A lot of user-friendliness is being achieved by picking a good default and hiding other options.

Provenance with transcriptions: for text in Korp, can we refer to the original image/audio? Can we link to individual words, with bounding boxes/timestamps.
Links are possible in Korp.
Gets more difficult with manually transcribed images (handwritten folklore).

Current state of handwritten text recognition is impressive; 2-3 pages enough for transcribing phonetic annotations.

Visualisation?

Hotspots for a search: where in the corpus does this occur?

Korp has a Google n-grams style diachronic comparison.

Some maturity required for visualisations to coalesce: some good ideas can come from playing, though.

Per Fallgren’s Edyson (https://github.com/perfall/Edyson) includes visualisation as a means of annotating a corpus; this could also be adapted to text.

Law text as a particular use case of text re-use.
Multiple layers can be possible: portions of the law can be cited in other texts, which themselves can be cited.
Text similarity tools can be helpful for these use cases.
