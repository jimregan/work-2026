## **1. Introduction**
Modern lexicography and speech technology development are increasingly reliant on large-scale spoken corpora that capture the complexities of natural speech. These corpora serve as foundational resources for linguistic research, automatic speech recognition (ASR), text-to-speech (TTS) systems, and various applications in human-computer interaction. However, the creation of phonetically rich and accurate spoken corpora presents several challenges, particularly when dealing with speech that lies between the poles of scripted and spontaneous discourse.
In the context of Swedish parliamentary speech, the nature of the data introduces unique complexities. Parliamentary speeches, especially those from the Swedish Riksdag, typically fall somewhere between read speech and spontaneous speech. While speakers often prepare scripts that form the basis for official transcripts, real-world delivery frequently diverges from these texts due to hesitations, false starts, mid-sentence repairs, prosodic variations, and other spontaneous speech phenomena. These divergences are critical for linguistic analysis and for the development of robust speech technologies but are often overlooked in corpora based solely on official transcripts.
This paper focuses on the development of a phonetically annotated corpus derived from Swedish parliamentary speeches recorded between 2012 and 2022. Our work began within the framework of the **SweTerror** project, which initially concentrated on analyzing the official transcripts of these speeches. However, as our research progressed, it became evident that the transcripts alone were insufficient for capturing the full range of speech behaviors present in the audio recordings. By employing ASR systems and forced alignment techniques, we aimed to explore the discrepancies between scripted and spoken language and to identify speech artifacts—such as false starts, hesitations, and spontaneous reductions—that are often critical for both linguistic research and speech technology applications.
The primary objectives of this research are twofold. First, we seek to create a phonetically annotated corpus that can serve as a foundation for the development of an improved phonetic dictionary with clear provenance. Existing phonetic dictionaries often contain inconsistencies and inaccuracies, particularly in their treatment of dialectal variations and spontaneous speech features. By grounding our dictionary in real speech data, we aim to enhance its reliability and utility for both researchers and practitioners.
Second, we aim to investigate the effects of speech rate on pronunciation. In many languages, including Swedish, faster speech is associated with systematic phonetic reductions that are not captured merely by speeding up the playback of pre-recorded or synthesized speech. Understanding these reductions is crucial for improving TTS systems and other speech technologies, which often fail to replicate the natural prosodic and articulatory patterns that occur in fast speech.
This paper is structured as follows: Section 2 details the data sources and initial motivations for the project, including the role of the **SweTerror** project. Section 3 outlines the methodologies employed, including the ASR system setup, forced alignment techniques, and approaches to phonetic annotation. Section 4 discusses the challenges encountered in developing the spoken corpus, with a focus on the complexities introduced by spontaneous speech features. Section 5 presents a case study on the impact of speech rate on pronunciation, highlighting implications for both speech science and technology. Finally, Sections 6 and 7 outline future research directions and summarize the broader contributions of this work.

## **Section 2: Data Collection Challenges (Expanded)**
The Swedish Riksdag provides a substantial and valuable source of speech data, encompassing over a decade of parliamentary debates, formal addresses, and committee discussions recorded between 2012 and 2022. This corpus presents a unique blend of read and spontaneous speech, offering rich material for both linguistic research and the development of speech technology systems. However, the process of collecting and preparing this data for phonetic analysis is accompanied by several significant challenges that must be addressed to ensure the integrity and utility of the resulting corpus.
**2.1. Origin and Nature of the Data**
Our initial engagement with the Swedish Riksdag speech data originated from the **SweTerror** project, which primarily focused on analyzing the official transcripts of parliamentary sessions. These transcripts serve as a formal record of parliamentary proceedings and are generally based on prepared scripts provided by the speakers. However, they often fail to capture the full complexity of spoken language, as they omit features such as disfluencies, hesitations, prosodic variations, and other speech artifacts that are crucial for phonetic and prosodic analysis.
Parliamentary speeches, while grounded in written scripts, frequently include deviations due to spontaneous speech phenomena. Speakers may diverge from their prepared texts for various reasons—emphasizing points, reacting to debates, or engaging in rhetorical flourishes. These deviations introduce elements such as false starts, repairs, fillers, and prosodic variations, which are often lost in the official transcripts but are essential for a complete understanding of spoken language behavior.
**2.2. Technical and Methodological Challenges**
**Audio Quality Variability:** The parliamentary recordings were captured in a setting not optimized for phonetic research. Variability in microphone placement, speaker distance, and ambient noise levels introduces inconsistencies in audio quality. Background noises, such as murmurs from other members, shuffling papers, and electronic interference, further complicate the extraction of clean speech signals. Additionally, overlapping speech segments—where multiple speakers talk simultaneously—pose challenges for accurate speech segmentation and speaker identification.
**Speaker Diversity and Variability:** The dataset encompasses speeches from hundreds of parliamentarians, each with distinct speaking styles, regional accents, and prosodic patterns. This speaker diversity enriches the dataset but also complicates phonetic annotation and automatic speech recognition (ASR). Variability in speech rate, intonation, and articulation requires ASR systems and annotation protocols to be flexible enough to accommodate a wide range of speech behaviors.
**Discrepancies Between Speech and Transcripts:** One of the most persistent challenges in using parliamentary data is the misalignment between the official transcripts and the actual spoken content. Transcripts often standardize or simplify speech, omitting spontaneous elements such as interjections, hesitations, and incomplete sentences. Moreover, speakers may improvise or rephrase their statements in real-time, leading to divergences that complicate forced alignment and speech-to-text mapping. These discrepancies necessitate robust alignment techniques and manual verification processes to ensure the integrity of phonetic transcriptions.
**Ethical and Legal Considerations:** While Swedish parliamentary data is publicly accessible, ethical considerations remain crucial, especially regarding speaker anonymity and data privacy. Although parliamentarians are public figures, certain discussions may touch upon sensitive topics. Therefore, care must be taken to ensure ethical standards in data handling, particularly when the corpus is intended for broader academic and technological dissemination.
**2.3. Strategies to Address Data Collection Challenges**
To mitigate these challenges, we implemented a multi-step data collection and curation process:
1. **Preprocessing for Audio Quality Improvement:** Signal enhancement techniques, including noise reduction algorithms and source separation methods, were applied to minimize background interference and isolate the primary speaker’s voice. This step aimed to improve the clarity and quality of the audio for both ASR and manual transcription tasks.
2. **Robust ASR System Development:** Recognizing the complexity of parliamentary speech, we fine-tuned a **wav2vec 2.0** model with a **Connectionist Temporal Classification (CTC)** loss function to improve its robustness against spontaneous speech artifacts. This approach allowed the model to better handle speech variations, though some limitations remained in capturing subtle prosodic shifts and overlapping speech.
3. **Manual Validation and Correction:** Given the inconsistencies between speech and transcripts, a subset of the data underwent manual validation. Annotators reviewed sections with significant ASR errors or alignment issues, correcting misrepresentations and ensuring the accuracy of phonetic transcriptions.
4. **Ethical Data Handling Protocols:** Clear guidelines were established for data handling, ensuring that the corpus maintained transparency regarding speaker identities while adhering to ethical considerations related to data privacy and responsible research practices.
**2.4. The Value of Parliamentary Speech Data for Phonetic Research**
Despite the outlined challenges, Swedish parliamentary speech offers a unique linguistic resource. Its blend of formal, semi-formal, and spontaneous speech modes enables analyses across a spectrum of linguistic phenomena, from scripted delivery to extemporaneous discourse. The dataset also provides insight into phonetic variation influenced by speech rate, sociolinguistic factors (e.g., regional dialects), and rhetorical strategies commonly employed in political speech.
By addressing the challenges inherent in collecting and processing this data, we aim to create a high-quality phonetic corpus that not only supports speech science research but also informs the development of more sophisticated speech technologies, including ASR systems capable of handling complex, real-world speech data.

## **Section 3: Data Curation and Preprocessing (Expanded)**
The transformation of raw parliamentary speech data into a structured, analyzable corpus involves several complex stages of curation and preprocessing. Given the inherent variability of spoken language, especially in contexts that blend scripted and spontaneous speech, this process requires meticulous attention to detail and the application of both automated and manual techniques. This section outlines the strategies and challenges involved in segmenting, normalizing, aligning, and annotating Swedish parliamentary speech data.

**3.1. Segmentation of Speech Data**
Accurate segmentation of continuous speech into linguistically meaningful units—such as utterances, phrases, and phonetic segments—is a critical preprocessing step. In parliamentary data, segmentation is particularly challenging due to several factors:
- **Spontaneous Speech Phenomena:** Disfluencies, hesitations, false starts, and mid-sentence repairs often obscure clear boundaries between linguistic units.
- **Overlapping Speech:** Parliamentary sessions frequently involve interruptions, interjections, and background noise, complicating the separation of individual speaker turns.
- **Pausing and Prosody:** Natural pauses in speech may not align with syntactic or prosodic boundaries, leading to mis-segmentation if relying solely on silence-based detection methods.
**Approach:** We employed a hybrid segmentation strategy that combines **voice activity detection (VAD)** algorithms with manual corrections. While VAD is effective for detecting speech presence and silence, manual intervention was necessary for handling complex cases, such as mid-sentence interruptions or background interjections.

**3.2. Normalization and Standardization**
Normalization ensures that non-linguistic elements and irregularities in the raw data do not interfere with linguistic analysis. In parliamentary speech, normalization addresses the following:
- **Disfluencies and Fillers:** Elements such as "uh," "um," and other hesitation markers were annotated but, depending on the analysis purpose, either retained or excluded from certain models.
- **Non-Speech Sounds:** Laughter, applause, coughs, and other paralinguistic sounds were labeled using standardized tags but were isolated from phonetic analysis datasets.
- **Code-Switching and Foreign Terms:** In multilingual parliamentary settings, instances of code-switching or foreign language usage were annotated explicitly, enabling focused phonetic analyses on specific language segments.
**Approach:** We adopted a two-tiered normalization process. Initially, automated scripts detected and tagged common disfluencies and paralinguistic events. Subsequently, trained annotators reviewed the flagged segments, ensuring consistency and accuracy in labeling.

**3.3. Forced Alignment and Time Synchronization**
Forced alignment techniques were used to map the phonetic transcriptions onto the corresponding audio waveforms, providing precise temporal boundaries for each phoneme, word, and utterance.
**Challenges in Forced Alignment:**
- **Spontaneous Speech Artifacts:** Forced alignment algorithms often struggled with spontaneous speech phenomena, such as hesitations, repairs, and informal contractions.
- **Transcript Mismatches:** Deviations between the official transcripts and the actual spoken words—common in parliamentary speech—led to misalignments, especially in segments with significant improvisation.
- **Inadequate Lexicon Coverage:** The forced alignment tools occasionally encountered out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words, particularly proper names and domain-specific terminology not present in standard phonetic dictionaries.
**Solutions Implemented:**
- We used a **customized lexicon** that expanded on existing pronunciation dictionaries to include parliamentary jargon, names, and common abbreviations.
- Alignment algorithms were fine-tuned with tolerance settings that allowed for minor deviations in speech, improving the system’s flexibility when handling spontaneous elements.
- In high-error regions, manual alignment was performed, especially in instances where the ASR system struggled with overlapping speech or rapid speaker changes.

**3.4. Metadata Annotation**
Rich metadata enhances the usability of the corpus, supporting more nuanced analyses of speaker characteristics, discourse contexts, and linguistic patterns.
**Annotated Metadata Includes:**
- **Speaker Demographics:** Age, gender, political affiliation, and region (where publicly available).
- **Session Context:** Date, type of session (e.g., debate, question time), and topic under discussion.
- **Speech Conditions:** Notes on audio quality, background noise levels, and technical issues affecting the recording.
**Annotation Protocol:** Metadata was structured using standardized schema (e.g., **IMDI** or **OLAC**) to facilitate interoperability with existing linguistic corpora. Annotators followed a comprehensive guideline to ensure consistency across the dataset.

**3.5. Quality Control and Validation**
Ensuring the integrity of the curated data was a central concern throughout the preprocessing pipeline. Given the scale of the dataset and the complexity of parliamentary speech, multiple layers of quality control were implemented.
**Validation Techniques:**
- **Spot Checks:** Random samples were reviewed by expert annotators to assess the accuracy of segmentation, normalization, and alignment.
- **Error Analysis:** ASR errors were systematically analyzed to identify recurring issues, such as misrecognition of parliamentary jargon or consistent misalignment patterns.
- **Inter-Annotator Agreement (IAA):** For subjective annotations (e.g., prosodic features or spontaneous speech markers), IAA metrics were used to measure consistency among annotators.

**3.6. Challenges and Lessons Learned**
The curation and preprocessing of Swedish parliamentary speech data revealed several broader challenges:
- **Balancing Automation and Manual Effort:** While automated tools streamlined many processes, the complexity of spontaneous speech required frequent manual intervention. Future work may focus on improving machine learning models to better handle these complexities.
- **Dialectal and Sociolinguistic Variation:** Differences in regional accents, speech rates, and rhetorical styles necessitated adaptive models and flexible annotation protocols.
- **Sustainability of the Corpus:** Ensuring that the curated corpus remains useful for future research requires clear documentation of preprocessing methods, annotation guidelines, and any modifications made to the raw data.

By addressing these challenges and adopting a multi-faceted approach to data curation, we aim to provide a high-quality, richly annotated speech corpus that supports diverse applications in speech science and technology. The curated dataset not only facilitates phonetic and prosodic analyses but also serves as a training ground for improving ASR systems and other speech processing tools in complex, real-world contexts.
