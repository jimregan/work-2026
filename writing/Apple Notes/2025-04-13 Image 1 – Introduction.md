## **Image 1 – Introduction**
**Explore your basic assumptions…** **Introduction** Here you'll learn more about your basic assumptions, rules for living and automatic thoughts. Your basic assumptions are the core of your self-esteem. Based on them, you set certain rules for how you should behave in different situations and what your self-worth is based on. Your basic assumptions and rules for living influence the thoughts that pop up in your head – your automatic thoughts.
The goal is for you to become more aware of your basic assumptions, rules for living and automatic thoughts. Once you know what they look like, you can more easily choose a different way to respond to them. This in turn can lead to greater freedom in what you can do and how you see yourself, others and the world around you.

## **Image 2 – Basic Assumptions**
**Basic assumptions** Your basic view of yourself, others and the world is usually called basic assumptions. They are a bit like the core or root of your thoughts. Your basic assumptions depend on your experiences. Positive experiences have a positive effect on your image of yourself and your worth, while negative experiences have a negative effect.
Your basic assumptions can be formulated in short sentences starting like this:
- I am ...
- Others are ...
- The world is ...

## **Image 3 – A collection of opinions**
**A collection of opinions** It is easy to think that the image you have of yourself and the world is true. Our brain processes a lot of information every second and needs to draw quick conclusions, which you can’t always question. This is fundamentally helpful, but has a downside if the conclusions are overly negative or inaccurate – which they often are with negative assumptions and low self-esteem. However, it is important to remember that the image you have of yourself is a collection of opinions that you have learned through your experiences.

## **Image 4 – Rules for living**
**Rules for living** Based on your basic assumptions, you create certain rules for yourself about how you should behave in different situations and how you judge your own value. We call these 'rules for living'. They can be very helpful when you need to quickly decide how to act, but they can create problems if they are too strict or unrealistic – which they often are if you have a negative self-image.

## **Image 5 – Examples of Rules for Living**
**Examples of rules for living**
Here are some examples of what rules for living can look like with low self-esteem:
- I have to perform to have value.
- I can’t rely on others.
- All my time must be spent on something useful.
- I must not get angry with my children.
Rules for living can also be formulated as conditions with the sentence “If … then …”
- If I do not perform, I am useless.
- If someone makes me a promise, I can expect to be disappointed.
- If I prioritize myself over others, I am selfish and others will start to avoid me.
- If I get angry with my children, I am a bad parent.

## **Image 6 – Strict and Unrealistic Rules for Living**
**Strict and unrealistic rules for living**
If you have low or fragile self-esteem, the rules become a way to function despite the negative image you have of yourself. If you follow them, you can feel better about yourself. But because the rules are often strict and unrealistic, they are very difficult to live up to. They can become self-fulfilling prophecies — the rules become proof to you that you are bad because you can't follow them.

## **Image 7 – Automatic Negative Thoughts**
**Automatic negative thoughts**
Your rules for living affect the automatic thoughts you get in situations. Automatic thoughts are thoughts that we don't consciously choose to think — they just pop up. If they are negative, they are called negative automatic thoughts. Strict rules for living often lead to many negative automatic thoughts.

## **Image 8 – Your Thoughts Influence What You Feel and Do**
**Your thoughts influence what you feel and do**
The thoughts you have in a situation in turn affect how you feel, physically and emotionally, and how you behave in the situation. In this way, your basic assumptions, life rules and automatic thoughts affect how you feel.

**Create new learnings**
Just as you have learned your basic assumptions, rules for living and automatic thoughts, you have probably learned to relate to them as truths to be followed. This means you can relearn — both in terms of what they look like and how you relate to them. You do this by testing what happens when you go against your assumptions, rules and thoughts and act differently. This creates new lessons. In the rest of the treatment, you will learn new ways to relate to and challenge your assumptions, rules and thoughts to gain more freedom in what you can do and how you see yourself, others and the world.

**Image 4:**
**Start with knowledge**
To make it easier to relearn, you need to become aware of your basic assumptions, rules for living and automatic thoughts and what they make you feel and do.
The thoughts, feelings and behaviors that arise on a daily basis are the easiest to discover, so we start there. They are also the ones you can work with concretely. When you learn how you relate to your thoughts, feelings and behaviors in everyday life, it will affect how you relate to your rules for living and basic assumptions.

**Exercise: Keep a diary of your thoughts**
Keep a diary of your negative automatic thoughts and what they make you feel and do for a week. If you find it difficult to find the thoughts, try using your emotions as a signal. When you realize that you suddenly feel a bit worse, you've usually had a negative automatic thought. Stop and think about what was going through your head seconds before. When you notice a rapid change in how you feel, write it down:
- **Situation:** What happens? When is it happening, where am I, who is involved?
- **Thoughts:** What am I thinking?
- **Feelings:** What emotions am I experiencing?
- **Behavior:** What do I do to deal with these thoughts and feelings?
Write this down immediately when the thoughts come or as soon as possible afterwards. Write at least three times a day for at least a week. This diary will recur and build up over the course of treatment, so it is good to practice it. The following pages give you some examples of what it might look like.

Here are two pages from Samir’s diary:
- **Situation:** The classmates talk about the past weekend during lunch.
- **Thoughts:** I didn’t do anything this weekend. If I say that, they will think I am the most boring person in the world.

- **Thoughts:** Ugh, I am so ugly.
- **Feelings:** Disgust, shame, contempt.
- **Behavior:** Looking down at the floor, avoiding eye contact, hurrying and buying food that looks "fresh".

**Page 14 of 23** **Exercise: Discover your rules for living**
Once you know a bit more about what negative automatic thoughts you have, you can move on to trying to discover your rules for living. You can do this by examining your thoughts using the following questions:
- Is there a recurring thought or theme?
- Do you find any rules in your thoughts about how you should be or act? They often include words like never, always, must or should.
- Do you find any conditions in your thoughts? They are often expressed as "If ... then ...".
Write down your answers.

- **Situation:** Working from home, receiving a message from my boss asking how I'm doing.
- **Thoughts:** What if my boss and colleagues don’t think I’m working hard enough?! Even though I work harder than many others.

**Page 18 of 23** **Exercise: Discover your basic assumptions** Once you know a bit more about what negative automatic thoughts and rules for living you have, you can move on to trying to discover your basic assumptions. You can do this by examining both your negative automatic thoughts and your rules for living. Use the following questions:
- What do your thoughts and rules for living tell you about how you see yourself?
- What do they tell you about how you see others?
- What do they tell you about how you see the world?
What you come up with here are your basic assumptions. Write them down. You can make your basic assumptions clearer to yourself by formulating them like this:
- I am …
- Others are …
- The world is …

**Page 21 – Proceed** Now you have become more aware of what your negative automatic thoughts, rules for living and basic assumptions look like. You may also have become better at recognizing how they affect you. Sometimes that realization alone can lead to change. If you haven’t been able to pinpoint them exactly, don’t worry – you don’t have to have perfect control. It may also be that this becomes clearer to you later in the treatment, so don’t think too long before moving on. We would like to remind you that the change does not come from thinking about your self-esteem, but from starting to act differently.
Don’t forget to take care of yourself while working on this. Since you have practiced thinking and acting in a certain way most of your life, the change may take time and be challenging.

**Page 22 – Summary**
- Your basic assumptions are the core of your self-esteem. They are a set of beliefs about yourself, others and the world that are shaped by what you have experienced and learned throughout your life.
- Your basic assumptions determine your rules for living. They are rules you set for yourself about how to behave in different situations.
- For people with low or fragile self-esteem, the rules are often unrealistic and overly strict, making them impossible to follow. The rules can become self-fulfilling prophecies – they become proof to you that you are bad because you can’t follow them.
- Your rules for living, in turn, determine the automatic thoughts you get. Automatic thoughts are thoughts that just pop up. If they are negative, they are called negative automatic thoughts. Strict rules for living often lead to many negative automatic thoughts.
- Your thoughts in turn affect how you feel, emotionally and physically, and your impulses (what you want to do). Your basic assumptions, rules for living and automatic thoughts can thus influence how you feel.
- If you want to change your automatic thoughts, rules for living and basic assumptions, it is good to first become more aware of what they look like. You don’t need to know exactly what they look like, but a little more awareness makes the next important step easier.
- The next step is to test what happens when you act differently and go against your assumptions, rules and thoughts. It will give you new lessons and more freedom.

**Page 23 – Your tasks**
1. Keep a diary during the week to discover your negative automatic thoughts.
2. Examine the automatic thoughts to discover what rules for living you have for yourself.
3. Examine your automatic thoughts and rules for living to discover what basic assumptions you have about yourself, others and the world.
