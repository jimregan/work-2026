For first-order perturbations of the uniform tract
$$\text{A}_o(x) = \text{constant}$$ $$\frac{\delta A(x)}{A_o} = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n \cos\frac{n\pi x}{L}$$ and $\left| a_n \right| \ll 1$ for all $n$. 

Under the assumption made thus far of no radiation from the lips, the formant frequencies are identical with the admittance poles. During actual speech, however, the formant frequencies will be slightly lower than the admittance poles as a result of the small, but non-zero, radiation load at the lips. In order to describe how the shifts in admittance poles (formant frequencies) and zeros are related to a given perturbation of the area function, it is useful to define a sequence of frequencies, $f_n$, which correspond to the ordered admittance poles (formant frequencies) for $n=1, 3, 5, \dots$, and to the ordered admittance zeros for $n=2, 4, 6, \dots$. Thus, in terms of the usual notation, the formant frequencies are given by $F_1 = f_1$, $F_2 = f_3$, etc. 

The relative shifts in the admittance poles (formant frequencies) and zeros away from their values for the uniform area function, $A_o$, arising from a small change in the area $\delta A$, are then related to the coefficients of the Fourier series in the following simple way: $$\frac{\delta f_n}{f_{on}} = -\frac{1}{2} a_n \quad \text{for } n=1, 2, 3, \dots$$ 
where $f_n$ is the $n^{th}$ frequency corresponding to $A(x)$, $f_{on}$ is the $n^{th}$ frequency corresponding to $A_o$, and $\delta f_n = f_n - f_{on}$. For the uniform tract, $f_{on}$ is known if the vocal-tract length is known. Thus, if both the admittance poles and zeroes are known, along with the vocal-tract length and the value of the area function at one point (or the average value), the area function is uniquely determined. That is,
$$\text{A}(x) = A_o \left\{ 1 - 2 \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left( \frac{\delta f_n}{f_{on}} \right) \cos\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) \right\} \quad (3)$$
