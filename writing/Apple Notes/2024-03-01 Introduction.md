# Introduction

Despite the recent trend in speech technology towards the use of end-to-end models, pronunciation lexica are still valuable assets, particularly in text-to-speech (TTS). In addition to technological uses, pronunciation lexica have various uses, such as their use as reference material in pedagogical areas, such as for language learning, or lexicography.

While other aspects of language data for technological, pedagogical, and reference purposes have shifted towards empirical, corpus-based methods, pronunciation data has typically resisted such trends: pronunciation dictionaries overwhelming tend to be prescriptivist in design, typically based on a standard version of a language, with little or no reference to other variants.

Parliamentary records in the modern age tend to be accompanied by a video recording, typically linked to the official version of the speech. 
In spite of the (perceived) limitation of typically conforming to a more formal register of a language generally,
parliaments, although limited to the dominant, official languages of a country, tend to have the advantage of being drawn from all of the areas of that country, leading to a greater diversity in terms of dialectal representation.

In this article, we investigate the use of parliamentary data from Sweden as a means of producing an acoustically validated pronunciation lexicon for Swedish, as a by-product of attempting to automate the correction of mismatches between the output of a CTC-based ASR system and the official transcript.
