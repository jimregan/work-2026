# Disentanglement vs factorisation


You don’t have to go far—the thesis points you there very directly.
## **📍 Where to look**
The key material is in **Chapter 1 (Introduction), Section 1.2:** ***“Approaches to Disentanglement”***
Within that section, the most relevant subsections are:
- **1.2.2 Factorizational Disentanglement** (pp. ~10–12)
- **1.2.2.2 Factorization in Speech**
- **1.2.3 Functional Disentanglement** (right after, for the author’s own stance)
## **🧠 Why this section matters**
This is exactly where the thesis:
- explains what *factorisation* means (separating information within a representation) 
- contrasts it with the author’s notion of *disentanglement* (which aims to **retain separated information**, not discard it) 
In fact, the distinction hinted at in the abstract is made explicit here:
- **Factorisation** → often *removes* variability (e.g. nuisance factors)
- **Disentanglement (in this thesis)** → *separates while preserving* all relevant factors

Read in this order:
1. **1.2.2 Factorizational Disentanglement** → what others mean by it
2. **1.2.2.2 Factorization in Speech** → how it applies in this domain
3. **1.2.3 Functional Disentanglement** → the author’s preferred definition
That sequence is essentially the conceptual expansion of the abstract’s claim.
