## §1 — INTRODUCTION **[ESSENTIAL]**

### What the paper is

This is a resource paper submitted to Language Resources and Evaluation (LRE). LRE resource papers are expected to: describe a resource clearly enough to be reused, argue for its scientific value, and include at least a basic demonstration of use. The paper is NOT primarily an ASR paper, a phonology paper, or a sociophonetics paper — though it draws on all three.

**Important framing note:** The project has always been oriented towards speech science; the phonetic focus is a refinement of that orientation, not a pivot away from ASR. The earlier published work (the "towards" paper, `old-towards.md`) addressed ASR for parliamentary data because reliable phonemic decoding is a necessary precondition for everything else — but the speech science motivation was present from the start. The introduction should convey this as a natural deepening of focus, not a rebranding.

**On the earlier texts as sources:** This paper has two predecessor texts that should be treated as quarries rather than blank pages:

- `old-towards.md` — the published ASR paper. Large sections can be reused directly or with light editing: the corpus description (the Riksdag data, the API, the metadata, the scale), the transcription methodology, the discussion of why parliamentary speech is valuable, and parts of the related work are all candidates for direct cannibalism. The main thing to strip out or heavily revise is the ASR-evaluation framing — the WER tables, the fine-tuning discussion — which belongs to that paper's argument, not this one.

- `XX-Old-intro.md` — from an earlier stream of the same project (thesis-oriented). The motivation sections are particularly relevant: the critique of end-to-end ASR as inadequate for phonetics, the argument about pronunciation dictionaries, the discussion of forced aligners as "better than nothing." These map directly onto what §1 and §2 of the LRE paper need to argue.

When writing any section, check both predecessor texts before writing from scratch. The blank page problem is often not actually a blank page problem here.

### The core claim

We have built a continuously growing phonetic infrastructure over approximately 60 years of Swedish parliamentary speech (Riksdag recordings). The infrastructure consists of three interlocking layers: a phone-aligned corpus, a living pronunciation lexicon with acoustically validated variant forms, and an inventory of phonological rules with acoustic evidence for each. Crucially, this is not a static release: the pipeline runs after each new parliamentary sitting, so the resource grows automatically.

### The problem motivating the paper

Pronunciation dictionaries — the standard way of linking orthographic words to their phonetic realisations — are built in a methodologically indefensible way. They target a single idealised dialect. They list canonical forms that may never occur in real speech. They contain large proportions of entries generated by grapheme-to-phoneme (G2P) rules, with no empirical verification. Users must take them at their word. This is in sharp contrast to how modern lexicographers work with meaning: corpus evidence is the gold standard for word senses, but phoneticians and speech technologists accept pronunciation inventories that have never been acoustically validated.

At the same time, the dominant speech technology paradigm (end-to-end ASR, Whisper-style) has moved away from phonetic representations entirely. This is fine for transcription, but it has left phoneticians, sociophoneticists, and speech scientists without tools that meet their actual needs. Forced aligners (Montreal Forced Aligner, Gentle) are widely used but widely criticised: they are better than nothing, but the error rates require prohibitive manual correction for careful phonetic work.

### What the introduction needs to do

It needs to establish: (a) why transcripts are not enough for speech science; (b) why pronunciation dictionaries are not enough either; (c) why parliamentary speech is a particularly good substrate for building something better; (d) what we built and why it is novel. The introduction should not be a literature review — that belongs in §2 (Related Work) — but it needs to gesture at the landscape convincingly.

### Open questions / things not yet resolved

- Exactly how to frame the relationship to ASR: the system uses ASR (wav2vec2/CTC) internally, but the paper is not about ASR performance. The introduction needs to manage this carefully or reviewers will expect ASR evaluation numbers we don't want to lead with.
- The "60 years" claim needs qualification. We have downloads going back roughly that far, but processing depth varies by era. What exactly can we claim about the earlier decades?
- The word "living" needs to be defined precisely enough to defend. Does it mean: the pipeline has run on new sittings? Or just that it could? Be honest about current state.

## §2 — RELATED WORK **[ESSENTIAL]**

### What belongs here

Three clusters of related work:

1. **Pronunciation lexicons and their limitations.** CMUdict, NST lexicons, the Swedish Lexin/SALEX tradition, work on pronunciation variation, lexicon induction from speech. The key point to make is that empirical validation of entries is essentially absent from the literature — most lexicons are either rule-generated or expert-judged, not acoustically validated at scale.

2. **Parliamentary speech corpora.** There is a reasonable literature here: Europarl (text-only but influential), various national parliamentary ASR efforts (Norwegian Storting, Czech parliament, etc.), the Riksdag work already published (the old "towards" paper). The key differentiator is that most parliamentary corpora are ASR training resources, not phonetic resources.

3. **Phonetic corpora and forced alignment.** TIMIT and its descendants, the Waxholm corpus for Swedish (important: this is the Swedish phonemic supervision tradition we build on), efforts to build phone-aligned corpora from broadcast/parliamentary data. The gap to establish: nobody has built a validated, growing, rule-grounded phonetic layer over a corpus of this scale and time span.

### What is not yet resolved

- Need a clear statement of what makes this paper's contribution different from just "we ran a forced aligner on parliamentary data." The answer is the validation loop — the acoustic hypothesis testing of phonological variants — but this needs to be stated compellingly enough that reviewers see it as a genuine methodological contribution, not just engineering.
- The Waxholm corpus connection needs to be described accurately. It is part of the Swedish phonemic supervision lineage that makes our CTC decoder work — but the exact relationship needs to be spelled out.

## §3 — CORPUS AND LIFECYCLE **[ESSENTIAL]**

### The substrate: Swedish parliamentary speech

The Swedish Riksdag has been recording parliamentary sessions since the 1960s. Audio is available via a public API. The recordings are broadly consistent in recording conditions — a formal chamber environment with close-mic setups — though quality varies across decades. Speaker metadata is rich: name, party, constituency, gender, role. This is crucial because it enables the sociophonetic angle: we can track individual speakers across years and link them to geographic and political variables.

The corpus is formally "parliamentary speech" but it is not purely formal read speech. Speakers often have prepared scripts but deviate in response to floor events. There are also reactions, interruptions, and informal exchanges that are not scripted. This gives the corpus an unusual register range for institutional speech.

Size: approximately 6,000 hours of video have been downloaded and processed for the core period (roughly 2012–2022 at depth); earlier material is available but processing is less complete. The total span with some coverage is approximately 60 years.

### The lifecycle: why "living" matters

Most speech corpora are released once and then frozen. This one is designed to grow. After each new parliamentary sitting, the pipeline ingests new audio, runs phonemic decoding, applies phonological rule expansion, validates variants acoustically, and updates the lexicon. Releases are intended on an annual cycle.

This is one of the clearest novelty claims. It should be stated plainly and defended: what does continuous expansion actually buy you that a large static release wouldn't? The answer has two parts: (1) longitudinal coverage — the lexicon reflects how pronunciation is changing, year by year; (2) sustainability — the resource doesn't become stale or require a major new grant to update.

### Open questions

- What exactly is the current state of processing for the pre-2012 material? This needs to be stated precisely. If the early decades are processed at word level but not phone level, say so.
- The "automatic expansion" claim: has this actually run on a new sitting since initial development, or is it a designed capability not yet operationalised? The paper should be honest about this.

## §4 — ANNOTATION AND VALIDATION PIPELINE **[ESSENTIAL]**

### The core mechanism: rules as acoustic hypotheses

This is the most technically interesting part of the paper and the hardest to explain clearly. The key insight is:

A canonical pronunciation lexicon (the citation forms) is taken as a starting point. Phonological rules — known productive alternations in Swedish (final devoicing, vowel reduction, assimilation patterns, etc.) — are applied to generate plausible variant forms for each lexical item. These are not added directly to the lexicon. Instead, they are treated as hypotheses.

The hypotheses are tested against acoustic evidence from the corpus. The CTC decoder (based on wav2vec2 fine-tuned on the Waxholm/Swedish phonemic tradition) produces a posterior lattice over phone sequences. For a given token in the corpus, we can ask: does the acoustic evidence support the canonical form, or one of the rule-generated variants, or neither? Variants that are acoustically supported above a threshold are promoted to the living lexicon, tagged with their rule provenance and acoustic evidence. Variants that are never acoustically supported inform rule refinement — the rule may be wrong, or context-conditioned in a way we haven't captured.

### What this achieves

- Pronunciation variants in the lexicon have acoustic evidence, not just rule-theoretic plausibility.
- The rule inventory can be evaluated: we can see which rules are productive, at what rate, and whether their productivity changes over time.
- The system is self-correcting: bad rules produce variants that fail acoustic validation and are flagged.

### Open questions — these are the hardest ones

- What is the actual threshold for "acoustic support"? This needs to be operationalised and defended. Is it a posterior probability threshold on the CTC lattice? A forced alignment score? This is where a reviewer will push hardest.
- How are the phonological rules represented and applied? Are they hand-written rules? Learned? A combination? This needs to be described precisely.
- What is the coverage of the rule inventory? How many rules, covering what phenomena? Are there known productive alternations that are not yet covered?
- False positive rate: what prevents the system from "validating" a variant that is actually a recognition error rather than a genuine pronunciation variant? This is a real methodological concern.
- The pipeline description says "phonemic recognizer / CTC posterior lattice" — but what is the actual decoding setup? Beam search? What vocabulary? This is technical but reviewers will ask.

### Active technical blocker

The reimplementation of `dysfluent-wfst` — the WFST-based component for handling disfluencies (false starts, hesitations, partial words) in the alignment pipeline — is currently being tested. An extension to it also needs to be verified. Crucially, the intended behaviour of the extension may have shifted since work on it a few weeks ago, so the first task is clarifying what it is currently supposed to do before testing whether it does it.

**Implication for the skill:** This is a real blocker, not a writing problem. Questions about the disfluency annotation layer (how false starts are detected and annotated, how the pipeline handles partial words) should be flagged as "this may depend on how dysfluent-wfst resolves." A useful question here is: "Before you can write the pipeline section confidently, do you need to settle what the current intended behaviour of dysfluent-wfst is?" — i.e., push the author to write down the spec, even informally, which may itself resolve the uncertainty.

## §5 — RELATED METHODOLOGICAL BACKGROUND: SWEDISH PHONEMIC SUPERVISION **[ESSENTIAL, but can be brief]**

### The Waxholm connection

Swedish has a relatively strong tradition of phonemically supervised speech resources, centred on the Waxholm corpus. This corpus was designed with phonetically rich sentences, broad phonemic transcription, and coverage of fillers and speech-noise events — an analogue to TIMIT but for Swedish. Our CTC-based acoustic model is grounded in this tradition: the phone set and transcription conventions derive from it.

This section exists to give methodological legitimacy to our phone-level representations. Reviewers unfamiliar with Swedish resources need to understand that our phonemic labels are not invented — they sit within a documented, peer-reviewed tradition.

### What is not yet resolved

- Exactly which aspects of the Waxholm phone set we use, and where we have deviated. Any deviations need to be justified.
- Whether to include the Swedish TIMIT-style coverage argument here or in §4.

## §6 — RELEASED RESOURCE LAYERS **[ESSENTIAL]**

### The three layers

**Layer 1: Phone-aligned corpus.** For each utterance: phone labels with timestamps, word boundaries, speaker IDs, session metadata. Also includes fillers, hesitations, false starts — not just clean lexical items. This is important: most forced-aligned corpora strip or ignore disfluencies; ours treats them as first-class annotations.

**Layer 2: Living pronunciation lexicon.** For each lemma: canonical (citation) form, attested variant forms with acoustic evidence scores, phonological rule provenance for each variant, token frequency, year-by-year attestation counts, speaker metadata linkage. The lexicon is queryable: you can ask which variants of a word are attested in a given decade, or by speakers from a given constituency.

**Layer 3: Rule inventory.** For each rule: a formal description of the alternation, the set of lexical items it has been applied to, acoustic validation rates (proportion of generated variants that pass acoustic validation), and temporal productivity (is the rule more or less productive in recent data?).

### What makes this feel like a genuine contribution

The multi-layer structure means a researcher can enter the resource at different levels of abstraction: a sociophoneticist wants the acoustic measurements; a computational linguist wants the lexicon variants; a phonologist wants the rule productivity data. This should be made explicit.

### Open questions

- Format: what formats are the layers released in? TextGrid? Praat? Custom JSON? This matters for reuse and LRE reviewers will ask.
- Licensing: is there any issue with redistributing the audio-derived layers given that the Riksdag audio itself is public? Need to confirm.
- Scale: how many unique lemmas in the lexicon? How many phone-aligned tokens in the corpus? These numbers need to be in the paper.

## §7.1 — USE CASE: PRONUNCIATION LEXICON GROWTH ACROSS DECADES **[ESSENTIAL — simpler use case]**

### What this demonstrates

This is the "show don't tell" moment for the living lexicon. Pick a set of lexical items known to have undergone reduction or change in spoken Swedish and show: (a) the canonical form; (b) the attested variants across decades; (c) the trajectory — are reduced forms more frequent in recent data? Does speech rate interact with variant frequency?

This is not a full phonological study. It is a demonstration that the lexicon captures real variation and real change. Even a small number of well-chosen examples (3–5 words with interesting variant trajectories) would be convincing.

### Open questions

- Which words to use? Ideally, words where there is existing phonological/sociolinguistic literature to anchor the description. Function words with known reduction patterns (Swedish "och", "det", "att", "inte") would be natural candidates.
- How to handle the confound of recording quality across decades? Earlier recordings have more noise, which might affect acoustic validation rates independently of actual pronunciation change.

## §7.2 — USE CASE: THE LIDINGÖ /iː/ PILOT **[NICE TO HAVE — flagship but optional]**

### Why this would be the best thing in the paper

The Lidingö /iː/ is a well-documented Swedish sound change: a distinctive vowel quality associated with upper-class Stockholm speech, historically stigmatised, that has undergone change over the decades. It is the kind of case where a corpus covering 60 years of Swedish speech, with speaker-level metadata linking speakers to constituencies near Stockholm, could in principle recover the trajectory of the change from real speech data.

This would be transformative for the paper because it turns a resource description into an independent scientific result: we didn't just build a corpus, we used it to replicate a known sociolinguistic finding. That is a strong LRE contribution.

### Why it is "nice to have" not "essential"

It requires: (a) identifying Stockholm-area speakers and appropriate controls in the metadata; (b) extracting stressed /iː/ tokens reliably; (c) measuring F1, F2, duration, possibly trajectory; (d) speaker normalisation; (e) showing that the acoustic pattern recovered matches what is documented in the sociolinguistics literature. Each of these steps is non-trivial, and if any goes wrong the result is misleading rather than absent.

If the pilot cannot be done cleanly, it is better to describe it as future work than to present a weak or confounded result.

### Open questions — these would be the best email questions to receive

- How are Stockholm-area speakers identified in the metadata? By constituency? By party? Are there enough repeated speakers with known Stockholm backgrounds to see individual trajectories?
- What is the operationalisation of the Lidingö /iː/? Is it a formant target, a formant trajectory, something else? What reference values does the sociolinguistics literature give?
- How will speaker normalisation be handled? Lobanov? Nearey? This matters a lot for comparing across speakers and decades.
- What is the expected direction of change, and over what time period? The paper needs a clear hypothesis before presenting results.
- What would a null result mean? If we don't find the expected pattern, is that because the corpus is wrong, the methodology is wrong, or the change is not present in parliamentary speech (a register effect)?

## §8 — CONTINUOUS EXPANSION PIPELINE **[ESSENTIAL — but can be short]**

### The sustainability argument

The pipeline loop is: new sitting → ingest audio and metadata → phonemic decode → rule expansion → acoustic validation → lexicon update → annual release. This is one of the clearest differentiators from existing resources. Most corpora require a new grant, a new project, and a new team to be updated. Ours is designed to self-update.

The key claim to make carefully: the pipeline has been designed for automatic expansion, and has been run on new material. The paper should be precise about the current operational status.

### Open questions

- What is the human-in-the-loop component, if any? Does the annual release involve manual quality checks, or is it fully automatic? What triggers a flag for human review?
- What is the latency between a new sitting and its appearance in the resource? Is this a bottleneck?

## §9 — FUTURE WORK: BACKEND MIGRATION **[NICE TO HAVE — can be one paragraph]**

### What this covers

The current backend uses wav2vec2-based CTC decoding. There are better options now: Gemma 4 CTC and similar models offer improved short-segment precision and better vowel boundary stability. The key point to make is that the validation loop is model-agnostic: swapping the backend does not invalidate the lexicon, because variants are tagged with their acoustic evidence, not tied to a specific model's outputs.

This is worth including because it answers the "won't this be obsolete?" objection directly. But it does not need more than a paragraph.

## PUBLICATION LANDSCAPE

This is not a single-paper project. The author is considering at least three outputs, and the skill should be aware of how they relate so that questions don't inadvertently push content into the wrong venue.

**1. LRE Journal — the main paper (this document's focus)**
The full living phonetic corpus and lexicon ecosystem. Resource paper. Peer-reviewed, archival. This is the primary target and the one these outline documents are for.

**2. arXiv pre-print and/or Fonetik submission — the "initial lexicon"**
The initial lexicon is an intermediate step in the pipeline: the pronunciation lexicon as it exists *before* acoustic validation — canonical forms plus rule-expanded variants, derived from the canonical citation lexicon and the phonological rule inventory. The raw data and code already exist; this is not a writing-blocked item but a decision about whether it is worth writing up separately at all. Fonetik is non-archival, so a submission there carries no risk. The question is simply whether the effort of writing it up is worth the return.
